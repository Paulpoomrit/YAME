- Data Collection

    For the ablation study, we compiled two sets of text, one human-generated (labelled H) and one AI-generated (labelled A). H consists solely of text from the Corpus of Contemporary American English (COCA), whereas A requires more processing. Here, we note that for each h in H, we want a corresponding a in A that is similar in content. One common approach (Solaiman et al., 2019) is to slice a portion of h and feed it directly to the generative model, prompting it to complete the sentence; however, this would thwart our attempt to highlight the distinctive features of AI-generated text. Therefore, we opted for a more explicit approach by adding an intermediate LM to “reverse engineer” the prompt from h (i.e., IntermediateModel(h) = p) and feed that prompt, p, to the LM we want to test.

    For instance, we may start with the following beautifully written text by Ursula K Leguin: h = *“We live in capitalism, its power seems inescapable — but then, so did the divine right of kings. Any human power can be resisted and changed by human beings. Resistance and change often begin in art. Very often in our art, the art of words.”* Now, we go on and prompt ChatGPT with the following:

    Consider the following text and imagine you were a human prompter, trying to reverse engineer the prompt of such text. Provide the prompt.

    Now, we’ve got the prompt p = “Write a concise, thought-provoking paragraph in the style of Ursula K. Le Guin that reflects on capitalism as a seemingly inescapable system of power, compares it to the once unquestioned divine right of kings, and argues that all human power structures can be resisted and transformed. Emphasize the idea that resistance and change often begin in art—especially in literature and the art of words.”

    Injecting this back to the LM, we’ve got simulacra of the original: *“We live within capitalism as our ancestors lived beneath the divine right of kings: as if it were weather, inevitable and unanswerable, a climate of power so vast that imagination itself seems to breathe by its leave. Yet the throne once crowned by God was dragged into the public square and named a human contrivance, and so too may this market-empire be seen for what it is—a story we are told, and therefore a story that can be retold. Power hardens when it is believed to be natural; it weakens when it is recognized as made. Every hierarchy speaks in the voice of permanence, but art answers in the tense of possibility. In poems, in novels, in the quiet rebellion of a sentence that refuses the given order, we practice other ways of being, and in that practice lies the first fracture in any empire. The art of words is not a mirror to power but a door out of it.”*
    The two texts were then saved into a csv file containing two columns, the first for the text in question and the second for the label (also H and A for humans and AI, respectively).
